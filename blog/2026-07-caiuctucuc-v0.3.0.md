# CAIUCTUCUC v0.3.0: The Rectangles Learn to Walk

*Draft for greenshoegarage.com, with release v0.3.0*

The game has pixels now. Real 16x16 tiles: rutted street, wave lined creek, timber walls with a roof course, stepping stones at the ford, plank docks. A character who is sixteen by twenty four with a four frame walk, a tricorn, and a coat that is actually the coat you are wearing, because the drover tan, the frock purple, and the preacher black are three separate sprite sheets and the disguise system picks between them. Change coats and you change on screen, which matters in a game where a coat is an alibi.

The day has a palette now, done the Super Nintendo way. The tileset is rendered four times through four lookup tables: day, dusk, night, fog. Same art, different light. Dusk goes copper, night goes blue and drops the values, fog eats the saturation. When the clock turns, the world does not get a tint smeared over it; the world is simply drawn from a different sheet, the way a 1994 cartridge would have done it.

After dark the lantern comes out: a darkness layer with a hole punched in it, and the hole's edge is ordered dithering, a proper 4x4 Bayer matrix, because the design doc has a rule that every effect must speak the 16 bit visual language even when the implementation is modern canvas compositing. And along the top of every outdoor screen, scrolling at quarter speed, is the ridge of Wills Mountain with a pale scar where the quarry bites it. The finale has been on screen since minute one. Now it is literally on screen.

All of this art is generated by a Python script, and that is the point of the milestone. The final art question, hand drawn versus commissioned versus licensed, is recorded as open in the repo, with exact slot specifications so any answer drops in without touching code. Forty five tests, including one that computes WCAG contrast ratios out of the stylesheet and one that measures the PNG headers to make sure every coat has its four frames.

The game looks like a game now. Next: pockets and workbenches.
